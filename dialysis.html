DIALYSIS

Dialysis is a medical procedure that is used to remove waste and excess fluids from the blood when the kidneys are no longer able to do so on their own. The procedure involves the use of a special machine that filters the blood, much like the kidneys, and returns it back to the body.


During dialysis, a patient's blood is first pumped out of the body and into a machine called a dialyzer, which is essentially an artificial kidney. The dialyzer filters the blood, removing excess fluids, waste products, and toxins. The clean blood is then returned to the body through a separate line.


Dialysis may be performed in a hospital or in an outpatient setting, such as a dialysis center. The procedure typically takes several hours and is often performed multiple times a week, depending on the patient's needs.


Dialysis in a hospital is a medical procedure that is performed on patients with kidney failure or other kidney-related conditions. Dialysis is a treatment that is designed to remove waste and excess fluid from the blood, much like the kidneys do when they are functioning properly.


In a hospital setting, dialysis is typically performed in a dialysis unit or center that is staffed by trained healthcare professionals, including nurses, technicians, and nephrologists. Patients may receive dialysis in a hospital if they are experiencing complications related to their kidney disease, such as fluid overload or high potassium levels.
